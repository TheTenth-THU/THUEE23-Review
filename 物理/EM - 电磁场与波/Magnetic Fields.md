## Generation of Static Magnetic Fields, 静磁场的产生

### Steady Currents, 稳恒电流

**Currents** represent the flow of charges, and **current density** characterizes the spatiotemporal distribution of currents.

> [!definition]- Current Density
> The **current density $\v{J}$** is defined as the amount of charge passing through a unit area per unit time, i.e.
> $$\v{J} = \frac{\dif}{\dif a} \dfrac{\dif Q}{\dif t} \vu{n}$$
> where the area element $\dif a$ is **perpendicular** to the direction of current flow, and $\vu{n}$ is the normal vector pointing out of the area.

^d1fde1

Consider a volume $V$ bounded by its surface $\partial V$. The total current flowing **into** the volume is
$$
\dfrac{\dif Q}{\dif t} = \dfrac{\dif}{\dif t} \int_{V} \rho\dif\tau = - \int_{\partial V} \v{J} \cdot \dif \v{a}
$$
where $\dif \v{a}$ is the vector area element pointing out of the volume. Thus, apply [[Math Basement#Gauss' Theorem|Gauss' Theorem]] to get
$$
\int_{V} \nabla \cdot \v{J} \dif\tau = - \int_{\partial V} \v{J} \cdot \dif \v{a} = \int_{V} \dfrac{\dif Q}{\dif t} \dif\tau
$$
which holds for any volume $V$. Therefore, we have the **continuity equation** for current density as

> [!theorem] Continuity Equation
> For any spatiotemporal point, the **current density $\v{J}$** and **charge density $\rho$** satisfy
> $$\nabla \cdot \v{J} + \dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0$$

^3a9af4

In magnetostatics, a stable magnetic field is generated by a **steady current**, which can be described as
$$
\dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 , \quad \dfrac{\partial \v{J}}{\partial t} = \v{0} , \quad \nabla \cdot \v{J} = 0
$$

### Biot-Savart Law, 毕奥-萨伐尔定律

The **Biot-Savart Law** (also called the **Biot-Savart-Laplace Law**) describes the magnetic field generated by a steady current. 

> [!theorem] The Biot-Savart Law
> For a **differential current element $\dif \v{I}$** at position $\v{r}'$, the magnetic field $\dif \v{B}$ at position $\v{r}$ is
> $$ \dif \v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{\dif \v{I} \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} $$
> where $\v{\sr } = \v{r} - \v{r}'$, $\dif \v{I} = I \dif \v{l}'$, and $\mu_{0}$ is the **vacuum permeability**. Under SI units, $\mu_{0} = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \rmu{H/m}$.

We can simply integrate $\dif \v{B}$ to get the magnetic field.

+ In the case of a **current-carrying wire**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{L} \dfrac{\dif \v{I} \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{L} \dfrac{I\dif \v{l}' \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2}
$$

+ For a **surface current distribution**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{S} \dfrac{\v{K}(\v{r}') \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} \dif a'
$$
where $\v{K}$ is the **surface current density**.

+ For a **volume current distribution**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}') \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} \dif \tau'
$$

> Consider a straight wire carrying a steady current $I$ along the $x$ axis.

### Magnetic Vector Potential  磁矢势

Noticing for volume current distribution, 
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \nabla \times \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' = \nabla \times \left( \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' \right) 
$$
we can define the **[[Magnetic Vector Potential|magnetic vector potential]] $\v{A}$** as

> [!definition] Magnetic Vector Potential
> The **magnetic vector potential $\v{A}$** is defined as
> $$\v{A}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau'$$
> where $\v{J}$ is the **current density**, and $\mu_{0}$ is the **vacuum permeability**.

so that 
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \nabla \times \v{A}(\v{r})
$$

## Properties of Static Magnetic Fields, 静磁场的性质

### Gauss's Law for B Field, 磁场高斯定理

From $\v{B} = \nabla \times \v{A}$ and [[Math Basement#Divergence of Curl|the property of divergence of curl]], we immediately have
$$
\nabla \cdot \v{B} = \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \v{A}) \equiv 0
$$

> [!theorem] Gauss's Law for B Field (differential form)
> The **divergence** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ at any point $\v{r}$ is zero, i.e.
> $$\nabla \cdot \v{B}(\v{r}) = 0$$

^1da827

By applying [[Math Basement#Gauss' Theorem|the divergence theorem]], we can convert it into the integral form.

> [!theorem] Gauss's Law for B Field (integral form)
> The **total magnetic flux** through any closed surface $S$ is zero, i.e.
> $$\oint_{S} \v{B}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{a} = 0$$

^411afe

### Ampère's Circuital Law, 安培环路定理

Consider the curl of $\v{B}$, we have
$$
\nabla \times \v{B} = \nabla \times (\nabla \times \v{A}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \v{A}) - \nabla^2 \v{A}
$$

For the 1st term $\nabla(\nabla \cdot \v{A})$, 
$$
\begin{align}
\nabla \cdot \v{A} &= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \nabla \cdot \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \nabla \cdot \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' \\
&= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \left(\dfrac{1}{\sr } \nabla \cdot \v{J}(\v{r}') + \v{J}(\v{r}') \cdot \nabla \dfrac{1}{\sr} \right) \dif \tau'  \\
&= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \v{J}(\v{r}') \cdot \nabla \dfrac{1}{\sr} \dif \tau' = -\dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \v{J}(\v{r}') \cdot \nabla' \dfrac{1}{\sr} \dif \tau'  \\
&= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{1}{\sr} \underbrace{\nabla' \cdot \v{J}(\v{r}')}_{0} \dif \tau' - \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \underbrace{\int_{V} \nabla' \cdot \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr} \dif \tau'}_{\textstyle\oint_{\partial V} \frac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr} \cdot \dif \v{a}}
\end{align}
$$
where $\nabla$ operates on $\v{r}$ and $\nabla'$ operates on $\v{r}'$. For $\displaystyle\oint_{\partial V} \frac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr} \cdot \dif \v{a}$, it is practical to consider a **large enough volume $V$** that all currents are enclosed, so that $\v{J}$ is zero on the boundary $\partial V$. Therefore $\nabla \cdot \v{A} = 0$, so the 1st term is zero.

For the 2nd term $\nabla^2 \v{A}$, we have
$$
\begin{align}
\nabla^2 \v{A} &= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \nabla^2 \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \v{J}(\v{r}') \cdot \nabla^2 \dfrac{1}{\sr } \dif \tau'  \\
&= \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \v{J}(\v{r}') \cdot (-4\pi\delta^3(\v{r} - \v{r}')) \dif \tau' \\
&= -\mu_{0} \v{J}(\v{r})
\end{align}
$$

Therefore, we have

> [!theorem] Ampère's Circuital Law (different form)
> The **curl** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ at any point $\v{r}$ is
> $$\nabla \times \v{B}(\v{r}) = \mu_{0} \v{J}(\v{r})$$

^7ae464

By applying [[Math Basement#Stokes' Theorem|Stokes' Theorem]], we can convert it into the integral form.

> [!theorem] Ampère's Circuital Law (integral form)
> For any closed loop $C$, the **line integral** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ around the loop is
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{l} = \mu_{0} \int_{S} \v{J}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{a} = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}}$$
> where $I_{\mathrm{enc}}$ is the **total current enclosed** by the loop $C$, and the positive direction of $I_{\mathrm{enc}}$ and the integration direction of $\dif \v{l}$ along $C$ are consistent with the right-hand rule.


> [!example] Magnetic Field of a Current-Carrying Stick
> Consider a **straight stick wire**, with radius $R$, carrying a **steady current $\v{J}_{0}$** along its direction. The **magnetic field $\v{B}$** at a distance $d$ from the center of the wire can be calculated by applying the **Ampère's Circuital Law**.
> 
> Consider a **circular loop** $C$ of radius $r$ centered on the wire with its plane perpendicular, we can integrate the magnetic field along the loop and get $\displaystyle\oint_{C} \v{B} \cdot \dif \v{l} = 2\pi rB$.
> + For $r > R$, 
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B} \cdot \dif \v{l} = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}} = \mu_{0} J_{0} \pi R^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad B = \dfrac{\mu_{0}J_{0}R^2}{2r}$$
> + For $r < R$,
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B} \cdot \dif \v{l} = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}} = \mu_{0} J_{0} \pi r^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad B = \dfrac{\mu_{0}J_{0}r}{2}$$
>

> [!example] Magnetic Field of a Solenoid
> Consider a **long solenoid** along $z$ axis, with radius of $R$ and turns of $N$ per unit length, carrying a **steady current $I$**. 
> 
> First, we can analyze the magnetic field **outside** the solenoid. 
> + By symmetry, conponents of $\v{B}$ along $s$ or $\phi$ directions are zero, so we only need to consider the $z$ component, which also needs to be **uniform** along the solenoid.
> + Consider a **rectangular loop** $C$ with length $L$ and height $h$, with its plane perpendicular to the solenoid. By applying the **Ampère's Circuital Law**, we have
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B} \cdot \dif \v{l} = B_{z}(s)h - B_{z}(s+L)h = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}} = 0$$
> So $B_{z}(s) = B_{z}(s+L) = \cdots = B_{z}(\infty) = 0$. Thus, **the magnetic field outside the solenoid is zero**.
> 
> Next, we can analyze the magnetic field **inside** the solenoid.
> + Simularly, by symmetry, conponents of $\v{B}$ along $s$ or $\phi$ directions are zero, so we only need to consider the $z$ component, which also needs to be **uniform** along the solenoid.
> + Consider a **rectangular loop** $C$ with height $h$, and with width of $L_{1}$ inside the solenoid and $L_{2}$ outside the solenoid. By applying the **Ampère's Circuital Law**, we have
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B} \cdot \dif \v{l} = B_{z}(R - L_{1})h - 0 = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}} = \mu_{0} N I h$$
> So $B_{z}(R - L_{1}) = \mu_{0} N I$, and $L_{1}$ do not affect the result. 
> 
> Thus, **the magnetic field inside the solenoid is uniform as $$\v{B} = \mu_{0}NI \vu{z}$$**
>
