Studying the $B$ field becomes more **intuitive** when compared to the $E$ field. One can observe a **strikingly parallel set of physical laws** governing both fields.

## Lorentz Force Law, 洛伦兹力定律

> [!theorem] Lorentz Force Law
> The **Lorentz force** on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\v{v}$ in a magnetic field $\v{B}$ is given by
> $$\v{F} = q \v{v} \times \v{B}$$
> The Lorentz force law describes how charged particles interact with electric and magnetic fields.

For charged particles, the **Lorentz force** would lead to the **circular motion** of the particles, thus the particles would move in a **spiral** path like **guided by the magnetic field lines**.

### Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Material, 磁场对电流的作用

A current-carrying material is a collection of **multiple charged moving particles**. The **Lorentz force** on a **thin wire** can be expressed as
$$
\v{F} = \dint_{L} \dif \v{F} = \dint_{L} \left( \v{v} \times \v{B} \right) \dif q = \dint_{L} \left( \v{I} \times \v{B} \right) \dif l 
$$
where:
+ **$\lambda = \dfrac{\dif q}{\dif l}$** is the **linear charge density** of the wire, and
+ **$\v{I} = \lambda \v{v} = \dfrac{\lambda \dif \v{l}}{\dif t}$** is the **current** flowing through the wire.

The Lorentz force on a current-carrying **surface** can be expressed as
$$
\v{F} = \dint_{S} \dif \v{F} = \dint_{S} \left( \v{K} \times \v{B} \right) \dif a
$$
where $\v{K} = \sigma \v{v}$ is the **surface current density** (current per unit width).

Most geneally, the Lorentz force on a current-carrying **volume** can be expressed as
$$
\v{F} = \dint_{V} \dif \v{F} = \dint_{V} \left( \v{J} \times \v{B} \right) \dif \tau
$$
where $\v{J} = \rho \v{v}$ is the **[[Magnetic Fields#^d1fde1|volume current density]]** (current per unit area).

## Biot-Savart Law, 毕奥-萨伐尔定律

The **Biot-Savart Law** (also called the **Biot-Savart-Laplace Law**) describes the magnetic field generated by a steady current. 

> [!theorem] The Biot-Savart Law
> For a **differential current element $\dif \v{I}$** at position $\v{r}'$, the magnetic field $\dif \v{B}$ at position $\v{r}$ is
> $$ \dif \v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{\dif \v{I} \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} $$
> where $\v{\sr } = \v{r} - \v{r}'$, $\dif \v{I} = I \dif \v{l}$, and $\mu_{0}$ is the **vacuum permeability**. Under SI units, $\mu_{0} = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \rmu{H/m}$.

We can simply integrate $\dif \v{B}$ to get the magnetic field.

+ In the case of a **current-carrying wire**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{L} \dfrac{\dif \v{I} \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2}
$$

+ For a **surface current distribution**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{S} \dfrac{\v{K}(\v{r}') \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} \dif a'
$$
where $\v{K}$ is the **surface current density**.

+ For a **volume current distribution**,
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}') \times \hat{\sr }}{\sr ^2} \dif \tau'
$$

### Magnetic Vector Potential, 磁矢势

Noticing for volume current distribution, 
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \nabla \times \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' = \nabla \times \left( \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau' \right) 
$$
we can introduce the **[[Magnetic Vector Potential|magnetic vector potential]] $\v{A}$** as

> [!definition] Magnetic Vector Potential
> The **magnetic vector potential $\v{A}$** is defined as
> $$\v{A}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\v{J}(\v{r}')}{\sr } \dif \tau'$$
> where $\v{J}$ is the **current density**, and $\mu_{0}$ is the **vacuum permeability**.

so that 
$$
\v{B}(\v{r}) = \nabla \times \v{A}(\v{r})
$$

## Properties of Static Magnetic Fields, 静磁场的性质

### Gauss's Law for B Field, 磁场高斯定理

> [!theorem] Gauss's Law for B Field (differential form)
> The **divergence** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ at any point $\v{r}$ is zero, i.e.
> $$\nabla \cdot \v{B}(\v{r}) = 0$$

By applying [[Math Basement#Gauss' Theorem|the divergence theorem]], we can convert it into the integral form.

> [!theorem] Gauss's Law for B Field (integral form)
> The **total magnetic flux** through any closed surface $S$ is zero, i.e.
> $$\oint_{S} \v{B}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{a} = 0$$

### Ampère's Circuital Law, 安培环路定理

Consider the curl of $\v{B}$, we have

> [!theorem] Ampère's Circuital Law (different form)
> The **curl** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ at any point $\v{r}$ is
> $$\nabla \times \v{B}(\v{r}) = \mu_{0} \v{J}(\v{r})$$

By applying [[Math Basement#Stokes' Theorem|Stokes' Theorem]], we can convert it into the integral form.

> [!theorem] Ampère's Circuital Law (integral form)
> For any closed loop $C$, the **line integral** of the magnetic field $\v{B}$ around the loop is
> $$\oint_{C} \v{B}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{l} = \mu_{0} \int_{S} \v{J}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{a} = \mu_{0} I_{\mathrm{enc}}$$
> where the positive direction of $I_{\mathrm{enc}}$ and the integration direction of $\dif \v{l}$ along $C$ are consistent with the right-hand rule.

## Magnetic Fields in Matter, 物质中的磁场

### Magnetic Dipole, 磁偶极子

#### Magnetic Dipole Moment, 磁偶极矩

Comparing the $B$ field of a small **current loop** with the $E$ field of an  **electric dipole**, we can define the **magnetic dipole moment** of a current loop as
$$
\v{m} = I \v{a}
$$
where $I$ is the current and $\v{a}$ is the area vector of the loop. The direction of $\v{a}$ and the current flow are consistent with the **right-hand** rule. The current loop is called a **magnetic dipole**.

$$
\v{A}_{\mathrm{dip}}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{\v{m} \times \vu{\sr}}{\sr^2}
$$
Then we can find the **magnetic field $\v{B}$ of a magnetic dipole** as
$$
\v{B}_{\mathrm{dip}}(\v{r}) = \nabla \times \v{A}_{\mathrm{dip}}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{1}{\sr^3} \left( 3 (\v{m} \cdot \vu{\sr}) \vu{\sr} - \v{m} \right) 
$$
And the **$B$ field of a magnetic dipole** under _spherical coordinates_ is given by
$$
\v{B}_{\mathrm{dip}}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{m}{r^3} \left( 2 \cos \theta \,\vu{r} + \sin \theta \,\vu{\theta} \right)
$$
where the dipole moment $\v{m}$ is in the direction of $\vu{z}$. 

The results above for an ideal dipole are **_identical_** to the **[[Electrostatics#Electric Field of an Electric Dipole, 电偶极子的电场|electric field of an electric dipole]]**. 

#### Magnetic Force upon a Magnetic Dipole, 磁偶极子上的磁场力

The force on a magnetic dipole is significantly more challenging to derive than [[Electrostatics#Electric Force upon an Electric Dipole, 电偶极子上的电场力|that on an electric dipole]]. 

To simplify the derivation, consider a magnetic dipole **formed by a square current loop** with side length $\delta$. 

Establish such a coordinate system that the **$B$ field at the center of the loop** is in the **$z$ direction**. To calculate the energy of the dipole in the magnetic field, we bring the dipole from **infinity** to $\v{r}$ position by 2 step:

> [!note]- Step 1: Bring the dipole from **infinity** to $\v{r}$ along the $x$-axis, with each side of the loop **parallel to the $x$-axis or $y$-axis**.
> Magnetic forces upon the sides parallel to the $y$-axis work. The **total energy** of the dipole is given by
> $$
> \begin{align}
> U_{1} &= U_{+} + U_{-} = -\delta I \dint_{-\infty}^{x_{+}} B_{z}(x) \dif x + \delta I \dint_{-\infty}^{x_{-}} B_{z}(x) \dif x \\
> &= -\delta I \left( \dint_{-\infty}^{x_{+}} B_{z}(x) \dif x + \dint^{-\infty}_{x_{-}} B_{z}(x) \dif x \right)  \\
> &= -\delta I \dint_{x_{-}}^{x_{+}} B_{z}(x) \dif x = -\delta I \cdot \delta B(\v{r})
> \end{align}
> $$

> [!note]- Step 2: Rotate the loop to align with the target $\v{m}$.
> Rotating the loop would **increase** the **potential energy** of the dipole, because such an operation makes it away from the **stable state**. The **energy change** is given by
> $$
> \begin{align}
> U_{2} &= \dint_{0}^{\theta_{0}} \tau(\theta) \dif \theta = \dint_{0}^{\theta_{0}} B(\v{r})I\delta \cdot \delta \cdot \sin \theta \dif \theta \\
> &= B(\v{r})I\delta^2 \left( 1 - \cos \theta_{0} \right) 
> \end{align}
> $$
> where $\theta_{0} = \langle \v{B}(\v{r}), \v{m} \rangle$.

Thus, the **total energy** of the dipole is given by
$$
\begin{align}
U_{\mathrm{M}} &= U_{1} + U_{2} = -\delta I \cdot \delta B(\v{r}) + B(\v{r})I\delta^2 \left( 1 - \cos \theta_{0} \right) \\
&= -B(\v{r})I\delta^2 \cos\theta_{0} = -\v{m} \cdot \v{B}(\v{r})
\end{align}
$$
and the **force** on the dipole is given by
$$
\v{F}_{\mathrm{M}} = -\nabla U_{\mathrm{M}} = \nabla \left( \v{m} \cdot \v{B}(\v{r}) \right) = \v{m} \cdot \nabla \v{B}(\v{r}) 
$$

## Magnetization of Materials, 材料的磁化

### Magnetization, 磁化强度

An external magnetic field can **affect the spin and orbital motion** of electrons in materials, which are all considered as **[[Magnetostatics#Magnetic Dipole, 磁偶极子|magnetic dipoles, 磁偶极子]]**, and lead to **extra magnetic dipole moments** in the material. 

> [!definition] Magnetization
> The **magnetization $\v{M}$** of a material is defined as the **magnetic dipole moment per unit volume**, i.e.
> $$\v{M} = \dfrac{\sum\limits\v{m}}{V}$$
> The **unit** of magnetization is $\mathrm{A/m}$.

### Bound Current, 磁化电流

For a magnetic dipole, $\v{A}(\vr) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \dfrac{\v{m} \times \vu{\sr}}{\sr^2}$. Therefore, for a **volume distribution of magnetic dipoles** $\sum\limits \v{m} = \dint_{V} \v{M} \dif\tau$, we have
$$
\begin{align}
\v{A}(\v{r}) = \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int_{V} \dfrac{\overbrace{ \nabla' \times \v{M}(\v{r}') }^{ \v{J}_{\mathrm{b}} }}{\sr} \dif \tau' - \dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \oint_{\partial V} \dfrac{\overbrace{ \v{M}(\v{r}') \times \hat{n} }^{ \v{K}_{\mathrm{b}} }}{\sr} \dif a'
\end{align}
$$
where we define the **volume bound current density** as
$$
\v{J}_{\mathrm{b}}(\vr) = \nabla \times \v{M}(\vr)
$$
and the **surface bound current density** as
$$
\v{K}_{\mathrm{b}}(\vr) = \v{M}(\vr) \times \hat{n}
$$
where $\hat{n}$ is the unit normal vector pointing out of the surface.

## Ampère's Law for H Field, H 场的安培环路定理

### Magnetic Auxiliary Field H, H 场

With magnetization, we can invoke the [[Magnetic Fields#^7ae464|Ampère's Law]] and get
$$
\nabla \times \v{B} = \mu_{0} \v{J} = \mu_{0} \v{J}_{\mathrm{f}} + \mu_{0} \v{J}_{\mathrm{b}} = \mu_{0} \v{J}_{\mathrm{f}} + \mu_{0} \nabla \times \v{M}
$$
i.e.
$$
\nabla \times \left( \dfrac{\v{B}}{\mu_{0}} - \v{M} \right) = \v{J}_{\mathrm{f}}
$$

> [!definition] Magnetic Auxiliary Field H
> The **magnetic auxiliary field $\v{H}$** is defined as
> $$\v{H} = \dfrac{\v{B}}{\mu_{0}} - \v{M}$$
> where $\v{B}$ is the _total_ magnetic field, $\mu_{0} = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{Tm/A}$ is the vacuum permeability, and $\v{M}$ is the magnetization. The **unit** of $\v{H}$ is $\mathrm{A/m}$.

### Ampère's Law for H Field, H 场的安培环路定理

> [!theorem] Ampère's Law for H Field (differential form)
> The **curl** of the magnetic auxiliary field $\v{H}$ is equal to the **free current density** $\v{J}_{\mathrm{f}}$, i.e.
> $$\nabla \times \v{H} = \v{J}_{\mathrm{f}}$$

> [!theorem] Ampère's Law for H Field (integral form)
> The line integral of the magnetic auxiliary field $\v{H}$ along a closed path $\varGamma$ is equal to the **total free current** $I_{\mathrm{f,enc}}$ passing through the surface $S$ enclosed by the path $\varGamma$, i.e.
> $$\oint_{\varGamma} \v{H}(\v{r}) \cdot \dif \v{l} = I_{\mathrm{f,enc}}$$

## Magnetic Boundary Conditions  磁边界条件

### For Normal (Perpendicular) Component  法向分量

On the direction perpendicular, 

+ For magnetic field $B_{\perp}$, $$B_{1\perp} = B_{2\perp}$$$B_{\perp}$ is always **continuous** across the boundary.

+ For $H_{\perp}$, since $\v{H} = \dfrac{1}{\mu_{0}}\v{B} - \v{M}$ we have $$H_{1\perp} - H_{2\perp} = M_{2\perp} - M_{1\perp}$$So $H_{\perp}$ is **discontinuous** across the boundary generally. 

### For Tangential (Parallel) Component  切向分量

On the direction parallel, 

+ For magnetic field $\v{B}_{\parallel}$, $$\v{B}_{1\parallel} - \v{B}_{2\parallel} = \mu_{0} (\v{K} \times \hat{n})$$where $\v{K}$ is the surface current density, and $\hat{n}$ is the normal vector pointing from medium 2 to medium 1. $B_{\parallel}$ will be **continuous** across the boundary if there is **no surface current**.

+ For $\v{H}_{\parallel}$, since $\v{H} = \dfrac{1}{\mu_{0}}\v{B} - \v{M}$ we have $$\v{H}_{1\parallel} - \v{H}_{2\parallel} = \v{K}_{\mathrm{f}} \times \hat{n}$$where $\v{K}_{\mathrm{f}}$ is the free surface current density. So $\v{H}_{\parallel}$ will be **continuous** across the boundary if there is **no free surface current**, or there is **only free surface current**.

### Magnetic Materials, 材料的磁性质

#### Paramagnetic Materials, 顺磁材料

In a **paramagnetic material**, the torque, 力矩, caused by the external magnetic field $\v{B}$ on the magnetic dipole **tends to align the magnetic dipole moment** with the external magnetic field.

![[Pasted image 20250423015910.png]]

For paramagnetic materials, the **susceptibility $\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$**, in [[Magnetic Auxiliary Field H#Magnetic Susceptibility and Permeability, 磁化率和磁导率|the relationship]] between $\v{M}$ and $\v{H}$, is **positive**, and the **relative permeability $\mu_{\mathrm{r}}$** is **greater than 1**.

#### Diamagnetic Materials, 抗磁材料

In a **diamagnetic material**, the orbital motion is much more rigid than the spin direction and is difficult to tilt. Instead, the electron would accelerate its motion and **increasing the dipole moment in a direction opposing** the external magnetic field.

![[Pasted image 20250423020527.png]]

For diamagnetic materials, the **susceptibility $\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$**, in [[Magnetic Auxiliary Field H#Magnetic Susceptibility and Permeability, 磁化率和磁导率|the relationship]] between $\v{M}$ and $\v{H}$, is **negative**, and the **relative permeability $\mu_{\mathrm{r}}$** is **less than 1**.

More specifically, the **superconductors** are the **perfect diamagnetic materials** with $\chi_{\mathrm{m}} = -1$ and $\mu_{\mathrm{r}} = 0$. Therefore, the **magnetic field $\v{B}$** inside a superconductor is **zero**. This phenomenon is called the **Meissner effect, 迈斯纳效应**.

#### Ferromagnetic Materials, 铁磁材料

**Ferromagnetic materials** have particularly significant **nonlinearity** in terms of the magnetization.

![[Pasted image 20250423021108.png]]

Also, ferromagnetic materials exhibit very **high magnetic susceptibility $\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$**, typically in the range of $10^3$ to $10^6$. Ferromagnetic materials exhibit strong magnetization **only below the Curie temperature** ($770 \rmu{{}^\circ C}$ for iron).
